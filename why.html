Why Offliner

You are not lazy.
You are outnumbered.

There are thousands of people employed to keep you scrolling, and one of you trying to stop. That is not a willpower problem. It is a fairness problem. This page explains what the scrolling is actually doing to you, what changes when you stop, and what Offliner does differently from everything you have already tried.

The difference

Accurate to the second.

This sounds like a small thing. It is the whole thing.

Most screen time tools round. They bucket your day into five or fifteen minute blocks, miss the short pickups entirely, and hand you a number the next morning that is close enough for a chart and useless for a decision.

Offliner reads directly from Apple's Screen Time system and reports to the second. Minutes on each app, number of pickups, number of times you tried to open something that was blocked. Live, while it happens, not in a report you get tomorrow.

The reason this matters is simple. A number only changes your behaviour if you believe it. The first time you see forty one minutes and twelve seconds on Instagram before eleven in the morning, something moves that a rounded "about an hour" never touches.

What it looks like

The symptoms nobody connects to the phone.

People describe these to a doctor as attention problems, or to themselves as getting older. Most of them have a much more boring explanation. Open the one that sounds like you.

You cannot watch a film without reaching for your phone

A feed delivers a new reward every second or two, and it never delivers the same one twice. A film delivers its payoff over ninety minutes. Once your attention has been trained on the first pace, the second one registers as a gap that needs filling. The reach for the phone is not boredom with the film. It is your attention looking for the rate of novelty it now expects.

The drive underneath: novelty seeking. We are built to investigate anything new because, for most of human history, new information was survival information.
You read a page and remember nothing on it

Scrolling rewards skimming. You scan a post, decide in a fraction of a second whether it is worth anything, and move on. Repeat that a few thousand times a day and skimming becomes the default mode your eyes run in. Deep reading needs the opposite: holding one thread long enough for it to move into memory. The words go in. Nothing sticks, because nothing was held.

The drive underneath: information foraging. The brain is optimising for the most information per unit of effort, and a feed has taught it that the cheapest information is the shallowest.
Things you used to enjoy feel flat

Your reward system does not measure pleasure on an absolute scale. It measures it against whatever you had most recently. When the most stimulating thing available is always in your pocket, everything slower gets graded against it and comes out looking grey. Cooking, walking, a conversation with a friend. None of them got worse. The comparison got unfair.

The drive underneath: relative reward. Anna Lembke's work on this describes the brain compensating for repeated high stimulation by pulling its own baseline down, which leaves ordinary life feeling muted until the baseline recovers.
You check your phone before you decide to

You are in a lift, a queue, a lull in a conversation, and the phone is already in your hand. You did not choose that. A habit that has been repeated a few thousand times stops running through the part of your brain that deliberates and starts running as a reflex triggered by a cue. The cue here is any gap at all. This is exactly why willpower keeps failing you: by the time willpower is invited, the app is already open.

The drive underneath: habit automation. Effort is expensive, so anything repeated enough gets demoted to a reflex, which frees up thinking for something else.
You cannot fall asleep without scrolling first

At some point the feed became your only wind down ritual, which is a problem, because it is a stimulant dressed as a sedative. You lie down tired, scroll for forty minutes, and arrive at sleep more alert than when you started. Then the eight hours you spend in bed do less for you than they should, and the next day is harder, and the harder day makes the evening scroll feel more deserved.

The drive underneath: escape. Scrolling is the lowest effort way ever invented to stop thinking about your day, so it wins by default at the exact hour you have the least energy to choose otherwise.
Being alone with your thoughts feels unbearable

This one predates the phone. In a set of experiments published in Science in 2014, researchers left people in a bare room for six to fifteen minutes with nothing to do but think. Most did not enjoy it, and a striking number chose to give themselves a mild electric shock rather than keep sitting there. The phone did not create the discomfort. It removed the last situations in which you were ever forced to sit with it, and a capacity you never use is a capacity you lose.

The drive underneath: avoidance of unstructured attention. Wilson et al., Just think: the challenges of the disengaged mind, Science, 2014.
The pattern in the data

It looks like ADHD because it acts like it.

In 2018, researchers followed around two and a half thousand teenagers in Los Angeles who started the study with no ADHD symptoms at all. Over the next two years they tracked how many digital media activities each one used at high frequency.

Among those doing none of them at high frequency, 4.6 percent showed ADHD symptoms at follow up. Among those doing seven, it was 9.5 percent. Among those doing fourteen, 10.5 percent. Roughly double, in twenty four months.

This is a correlation, not a proven cause, and the study says so plainly. But the direction is consistent, it held after adjusting for the obvious confounders, and it matches what people describe to us in their own words before they have seen a single chart.

Ra et al., Association of Digital Media Use With Subsequent Symptoms of ADHD Among Adolescents, JAMA, 2018.

Before and after

What actually changes.

Not a personality transplant. A baseline moving back to where it started.

Before
  • The phone is in your hand before you notice reaching for it
  • Quiet feels like something has gone wrong
  • You need a second screen to get through the first one
  • Slow things feel like waiting rather than living
  • You know your screen time number and you avoid looking at it
After
  • You put the phone down and forget where you left it
  • Quiet stops being uncomfortable and starts being where thinking happens
  • One thing at a time stops feeling like a sacrifice
  • A book holds you for an hour without effort
  • You check the number because you are curious, not because you are bracing

The baseline, drawn

Heavy stimulation pulls your reward baseline below where it started, which is why ordinary things feel flat while you are still in it. Take the stimulation away and the baseline climbs back. The first week is the worst part and the most productive part at the same time.

The hard part

A bad day is not a failure. It is the dose.

Nobody warns you about this part, so people quit on day three thinking it is not working, when day three feeling terrible is the clearest evidence that it is.

The first week is restless. You will reach for a phone that is not there. You will be irritable in a way you cannot quite justify. You will be convinced, briefly and with total sincerity, that you were fine before and this is all a bit much. That is withdrawal, and it is not a sign you are doing it wrong. It is a sign there was something to withdraw from.

It is also front loaded. The discomfort is worst at the start and drops away faster than the benefit arrives, which means the effort curve and the reward curve run in opposite directions. Almost everyone who quits, quits in the window where they have paid the full price and not yet collected.

So: if today was bad, today counted. Offliner is built around this. A broken day leaves a blank square in your week and the streak starts again. It does not delete your history, it does not lecture you, and it does not pretend the day did not happen.

The process

What the first month feels like.

Everyone is different, and this is a map rather than a promise. It is here so that when day four is unpleasant, you know day four is meant to be unpleasant.

Day 1

Relief, then reaching

Setting it up feels good. Then you catch yourself opening a blocked app maybe fifteen or twenty times without deciding to, which is the first honest measurement you have ever had of the habit.

Days 2 to 4

The worst of it

Restlessness, a short fuse, and a very convincing internal argument that you did not really need to do this. Attempted opens peak here. So does the urge to delete the app.

Days 5 to 7

The gaps start filling themselves

You reach for the phone, remember, and put it down without much drama. Something else moves into the space: a book, a walk, an actual conversation. It is not exciting yet. It is just easier.

Week 2

Attention comes back before you notice

You read for an hour and it does not hurt. You sit through a film. Someone tells you something and you are still there at the end of the sentence.

Week 3

Ordinary things stop being flat

The comparison has reset. Cooking is interesting again. Sleep works properly. The streak becomes the thing you are protecting, and protecting it stops requiring effort.

Week 4 and after

You stop thinking about it

The apps are still on your phone. You open them sometimes. The difference is that you decide to, and then you stop, and stopping is not a negotiation.

From the beta 4out of 5

Four times out of five, when a blocked app came up against a real alternative, beta users chose the alternative. Reading. Training. Cooking. Going outside. Calling someone back.

This is the part that surprised us. People do not need to be talked out of scrolling. They need the scroll to stop being the path of least resistance for a few seconds, which is exactly how long the decision takes.

The last place you were ever alone with your own thoughts was probably a queue in 2011.

Every gap that used to belong to your own mind has been filled. The lift, the bus, the three minutes before a meeting, the walk to the shop, the ninety seconds waiting for the kettle. Those gaps were not wasted time. They were where you worked out what you actually thought about things.

Ideas need somewhere to arrive. Nobody has ever had a good one while scrolling, and almost everybody can name one they had in the shower, which is the last remaining place on earth where you cannot take your phone.

Giving those gaps back is not a side effect of Offliner. It is the point. The blocking is just the mechanism.

Everything in the app

What you get.

Streak

Distraction free days, counted from day one. Detox days always count.

Focus Score

A daily score out of one hundred, weighted so a slip shows up immediately and a bad day does not bottom out.

Reclaimed

Cumulative hours won back against your own honest baseline.

Rules

Work hours, bedtime, mornings, weekends, or build your own by day, time window and app.

Detox

Full lockout for a set period. The apps do not open at all, so the streak cannot break.

Limits

Daily minute or pickup allowances per app, with a live bar showing what is left.

Focus sessions

Timer or pomodoro, named and saved, with the whole phone quiet until it ends.

Live Activity

Session countdown on the Lock Screen and in the Dynamic Island.

Insights

Day, week and month views, most used apps, peak hours, and recommendations you can apply as a rule.

Friction screens

Your real numbers at the moment you try to open something blocked. No instant unlock anywhere.

Private by default

Everything is computed on device. No account, no upload, nothing to leak.

iPhone and iPad

One purchase, both devices, synced through your own iCloud.

Journal

Longer reads.

Short, occasional, and only when there is something worth saying.
