Comparison

The best screen time app for iPhone, honestly compared.

I build one of the apps on this page, so read it with that in mind. I have tried to write the comparison I wanted to find when I was the one searching, which means saying plainly where the others are better and where mine is not the right choice.

Start here

Most comparisons ask the wrong question.

Nearly every "best screen time app" list ranks on features. Number of blocking modes, prettiness of the charts, whether there is a widget. That is the wrong axis, because almost nobody fails at reducing their screen time due to a missing feature.

People fail at eleven at night, holding the phone, when the app in front of them offers a way through and they take it. So the only question that predicts whether a screen time app will work for you is this: how much does it cost you, in seconds and in consequences, to talk yourself past it?

Everything below is organised around that. If you have never tried anything, start with Apple's built in Screen Time, because it is free and it works for a meaningful number of people. If you have already bypassed it, you now know something useful about yourself, and you should choose based on friction rather than features.

In detail

When each one is the right answer.

Apple Screen Time

Free, built in, and the correct first thing to try. It covers app limits, downtime and content restrictions, and for people whose usage is a mild habit rather than a compulsion, it is often enough.

It fails for a specific reason. The block screen has an "Ignore Limit" button on it. That is roughly one second of friction and no consequence at all, and a limit you can dismiss in one tap is a suggestion. It also rounds your usage and reports it the next morning, which is too late and too vague to change a decision you are making right now.

Choose it if: you have never set a limit before. It costs nothing to find out.

one sec

Rather than blocking, one sec interrupts. When you open a chosen app it runs a short breathing exercise before letting you through. The idea is grounded in behavioural research and it works better than it sounds, because most app opens are reflex rather than intention, and a reflex interrupted often does not resume.

Its limitation is that it is not designed to enforce a ceiling. If you genuinely intend to scroll for an hour, one sec will let you, having asked you politely first.

Choose it if: your problem is picking the phone up without deciding to, rather than total hours.

ScreenZen

The best free third party option. It adds a configurable delay before a chosen app opens, anywhere from five seconds to several minutes, and it is genuinely capable rather than a stripped back trial.

The catch is that you set the delay yourself and you can change it yourself, which means the friction is only as strong as your mood when you last opened the settings.

Choose it if: budget is the constraint. It is a real tool, not a demo.

Opal

The most polished product in the category and the best analytics if you want to understand your patterns in depth. If you enjoy looking at your own data, Opal gives you the most to look at.

It is also the most expensive by a distance, and depth of analytics is not the same thing as difficulty of bypass. Some of its modes are strict and some are not, so what you get depends heavily on how you configure it.

Choose it if: you want the most refined experience and price is not the deciding factor.

Freedom

The only one that genuinely solves cross device. Start a session and it blocks simultaneously on your iPhone, iPad, Mac, Windows PC and browser. If your problem moves to whatever screen is nearest, this is the one that closes that gap.

On iPhone specifically it is not the strictest option, and its measurement is basic. It is solving a different problem, and solving it well.

Choose it if: you block on your phone and then immediately open the same site on your laptop.

Offliner

Mine, so discount accordingly. It is built around two decisions. First, there is no instant unlock anywhere: the secondary button on a block screen starts a live fifteen second countdown, then grants five minutes, and taking it breaks your streak. Second, the block screen shows your real numbers rather than a padlock. How many times you have tried to open this app today, how long you have already spent in it, how long your streak is.

Those numbers are measured to the second rather than rounded, which is the part I care most about. An estimate is easy to argue with and an exact figure is not.

Do not choose it if: you need Windows or Android, you want the deepest analytics dashboard, or you have never tried anything and Apple's free tool might work.

Decision guide

Pick based on how you failed last time.

1

You have never tried anything

Use Apple Screen Time. It is free, already installed, and for a real share of people it is sufficient. Find out before you pay for anything.

2

You tap Ignore without thinking

You need consequence, not just a barrier. Look for a timed wait you cannot skip and something you lose by using it. That is the design Offliner is built around.

3

You open apps without deciding to

Your problem is the reflex rather than the hours. An interrupt like one sec, or a short configurable delay like ScreenZen, targets exactly that.

4

You switch to your laptop

Blocking one device just relocates the problem. Freedom is the only option here that genuinely covers everything at once.

5

You do not believe your own numbers

If a rounded daily estimate has stopped meaning anything to you, accuracy is the lever. Look for live, per second measurement rather than a next morning summary.

6

You deleted the app instead

Deleting works for about four hours. Reinstalling takes eleven seconds. You need something that survives the version of you who reinstalls, which means friction plus something at stake.

A note on bias

I am not a neutral source and neither is anyone else writing these.

Nearly every "best screen time app in 2026" article you will find is published by one of the apps in it, and most of them do not say so. This one is published by Offliner and Offliner comes out well in it, which is exactly what you would expect and exactly why you should not take my word alone.

What I can offer is falsifiable claims. Apple Screen Time really does have an Ignore Limit button. Freedom really is the only one covering Windows. Opal really is the most expensive. Every one of those is checkable in about a minute, and if any of them stops being true, this page is wrong and you should tell me at info@offliner.app.

The best test is still the free one. Almost everything here has a free tier or a trial. Install two, use them for a week, and keep whichever one you have not yet found a way around.
